Context

This interview discusses themes of place, terroir, community food systems, and how cooking can act as both adaptation and preservation.

Interview Questions/Excerpts

Are there any dishes or ingredients that are particularly meaningful to you? Why?

My cooking and writing are defined by a deep commitment to terroir, the unique taste of a specific place. I focus on ingredients that are native to or sustainably produced in Southwestern Ontario. My work aims to revive forgotten regional flavors using heritage ingredients like pawpaw, quince, and Jerusalem artichokes, while working closely with local farmers and producers.

Are there any dishes you feel are especially tied to your culinary identity or background?

My cooking reflects a career spent bridging classical French training with the seasonal rhythms of Southwestern Ontario. Dishes like a locally inspired tourtière show how I combine tradition with regional ingredients, shaped by both my training and my experiences.

Do you think of your cooking as connected to any cultures, heritage or communities?

My work is deeply connected to the Southwestern Ontario agricultural community, the Slow Food movement, and a broader belief in culinary multiculturalism as part of Canadian identity.

How does cooking connect you to your community?

Moving away from strict French technique toward regional cooking led me to a philosophy where food is inseparable from the geography and traditions of the people who produce it.

Do you find cooking as something that brings people together, in what ways?

A true taste of place is impossible to achieve alone. It requires collaboration between the land, the producer, the chef, and the diner.

Do you ever find yourself having to adapt or change dishes because of ingredients, location or audience?

My entire philosophy is built on adaptation. A static menu fails to engage with the seasons and the needs of the community.

Do you think these adaptations can change the meaning of a dish?

The plate is a vessel for storytelling. Changing a dish doesn’t just affect flavor — it changes the narrative behind it.

Do you see cooking as a way of preserving something important to you or others?

Cooking is a form of cultural and environmental preservation. It helps protect regional identity and supports local producers against industrialization.

Commentary

For Bryan, cooking begins with a commitment to place. His focus on local and heritage ingredients reflects an understanding of food as something rooted in specific environments, where taste is shaped by land, climate, and regional practices.

Anthropologist Tim Ingold suggests that people develop knowledge through their relationship with the environments they inhabit. This helps explain Bryan’s emphasis on terroir. Ingredients like pawpaw, quince, and Jerusalem artichokes are not just selected for taste, but for their connection to Southwestern Ontario.

Bryan’s work also highlights the importance of local food systems. His relationships with regional producers show how cooking depends on networks of farmers, artisans, and suppliers. Goodman and colleagues argue that these systems offer an alternative to industrialized food by emphasizing sustainability and community connection.

This is evident in Bryan’s approach, where cooking is not an individual act but a collaborative process. A “true taste of place” emerges through the combined efforts of producers, chefs, and diners.

At the same time, Bryan sees cooking as something that must constantly adapt. Seasonal changes and ingredient availability shape what appears on the plate. As Mintz and Du Bois suggest, food evolves in response to social and economic conditions.

For Bryan, this evolution is not a loss of authenticity, but part of the story. The plate becomes a “vessel for storytelling,” where each adaptation reflects a changing context, balancing preservation with change.
