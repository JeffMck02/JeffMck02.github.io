    Context

    Interview Questions/Excerpts

    Did growing up in Serbia help shape how you understand food?

    “Big time, yeah. We grew up on a family farm. We raised our own livestock and grew most of our vegetables, but we were also surrounded by farmers and markets. So the understanding of buying fresh produce and eating things that are in season was implemented very quickly for us. Strawberries grow once a year, and that’s it…after that you wait until next year. Coming here was a big shock, seeing strawberries available twelve months a year. Back home, when something was in season, that was what you ate. After that, you preserved it, you made jams, marmalades, things like that and waited for the next season. Cooking was a big part of our house. My mom made meals every day, and we were always involved in that process.” CONT. “My mom was a classic housewife who really taught us and got us involved in cooking. My father was in charge of the harvest and livestock, processing the meat and making things like sausages. That was just standard life back home, it wasn’t professional, it was just how you lived.”

    Are there any dishes or ingredients that are particularly meaningful to you? Why?

    “It really depends on the season. In winter, sauerkraut is huge. We make our own preserved cabbage. Peppers are also a big part of the diet, from fresh to preserved, made into sauces, dips like ajvar. We also made our own fruit brandy as a family. As kids we didn’t drink it, but we were part of the process and learned how it was made. Bread was baked once a month in a wood oven outside. That was just how things were done.”

    Are there any cooking traditions that you feel committed to maintaining?

    “We are very particular with family gatherings. Christmas is huge. Christmas is in January and it involves several days of preparation and celebration. There’s fasting before, then big gatherings where neighbors come together, food is made in the streets, and you celebrate for several days with family and friends. We still keep those traditions here in Canada with my family.”

    Do you find cooking as something that brings people together, in what ways?

    “The favorite part is the gathering. People get tired of cooking sometimes, but when you have friends over, it becomes something else. It becomes about sharing, making a feast for everyone. It should be about food and friendship together.”

    Does cooking connect you to home?

    “When I go back home, I’m always surprised by how different simple ingredients taste, even something like a tomato. The flavors are very different. Some things are better here, some are better there. I don’t necessarily cook traditional dishes in the restaurant, but those influences stay with me when I create menus.”

    Commentary

    Dacha’s experience growing up on a family farm reflects a way of relating to food that is rooted in daily life. Food was often not purchased but produced through work, raising livestock, growing produce, preparing the food for meals as part of a larger routine. Anthropologist Tim Ingold suggests that we don’t stand apart from the environments we inhabit, both shaping the environment and being shaped by it simultaneously, in this context food is not just something we consume but also experience as part of an ongoing relationship with the land we inhabit. In their book critiquing modern agricultural practices The Unsettling of America Wendell Berry argues for a fundamental divide in how people relate to land, distinguishing between exploitation and nurture. While an exploitative approach is concerned mainly with efficiency and high output, a nurturing approach is associated with care for the land, continuity and a long term vision of sustainability. This distinction helps frame Dacha’s experience on the family farm and his relationship with food as something based on caring for and establishing a connection with the land over a long period of time. As Berry suggests, this kind of relationship hinges on understanding that “we and our land are part of one another” rather than separate entities working toward our own ends. What Dacha describes reflects this integrated way of living, where food, labor and the environment we inhabit are inseparable, and where cooking is part of a broader network of care for both the community and the land.

    The way Dacha describes food like sauerkraut, ajvar, and homemade bread reflects a system of cooking that is both shaped by necessity and long-standing, time honored tradition. These foods aren’t based on regional preferences but adaptation to seasonal availability and the need to preserve ingredients over time. What stands out here is that these practices are not isolated or individual, but part of a widespread regional pattern. Research done by Soukland, Et al. on eastern european food systems show that fermented food like sauerkraut have been widely produced in domestic settings for generations, this plays a large role in the formation of shared environmental and cultural knowledge. At the same time, these practices are inherently social. As Carole Counihan emphasizes in Anthropology of Food and Body, food preparation most often takes place in shared settings, where knowledge is passed down through participation and collective activity rather than instruction you would see in a professional setting. Cooking becomes something learned by doing and sharing alongside others, rather than individually. This encapsulates what Dacha is describing. Making preserved foods, baking bread and producing things like fruit brandy become shared processes that involve multiple generations. These activities become about connecting the seasonal rhythms, family life, and cultural tradition passed down through generations via everyday acts of cooking.

    Dacha’s description of Orthodox Christmas highlights the ways in which food becomes a part of larger systems of ritual and tradition. Celebrated in countries such as: Russia, Ukraine, Georgia and Serbia according to the Julian calendar, the holiday is preceded by a period of fasting and followed by several days of communal cooking and shared meals. What stood out to me in Dacha’s account is the scale and structure of the celebration. Food extends beyond the household and into the community, where neighbors gather, cook together and celebrate. Anthropologist E.N Anderson emphasizes how food practices are part of a much broader cultural system shaped by social interaction, environment, and shared knowledge. Meals are not isolated, instead they reflect how communities are organized and express shared values. At the same time, holidays mark a shift from the foods we eat in our everyday lives. In her work on Tuscan food traditions, Carole Counihan talks about how holidays and special occasions are often defined by foods that are otherwise rare, unavailable or unique to the celebration, which make these meals distinct from daily life. This helps exemplify the significance of what Dacha describes. The change from fasting to feasting, from ordinary meals to large communal gatherings, transforms food into a celebration. The meals don’t simply accompany the holiday, but help define it. Even in Canada, continuing these traditions as Dacha talks about allows those meanings to carry on, showing how food can carry ritual, identity, and community across different places and times.

    To Dacha, cooking isn’t just about the preparation of food, but creating opportunities for people to come together. While cooking itself can feel like work, many find that it takes on a different meaning when it becomes part of a shared gathering or event. In his work, Claude Fischler talks a lot about eating together being a central aspect of human social life, commensality is our tendency to eat in groups, where meals become about creating a shared experience and strengthening relationships rather than just feeding ourselves. This can be seen in Dacha’s reflection, where cooking becomes about a collective, a way of making a feast for others and bringing people together. Carole Counihan also notes that food plays a central role in not just creating relationships but maintaining them as well, creating a space where people can connect through a shared experience or activity. This was a common theme I noticed among my interviews as well, and I'm sure it's something that many people can relate to.

    For Dacha, the connection between food and home is less about specific dishes and more about things like taste and experience. Even something so simple like a tomato can feel or taste different depending on where it’s grown, I found this to really emphasize how closely flavor is tied to place, this theme is also explored in another one of my interviews. As David Sutton also suggests, food and memory are deeply intertwined, with taste often triggering strong associations or memories of particular places or moments. These sensory experiences create lasting connections to home or other important places, even when you’re very far away from that place. This helps frame Dacha’s reflection on the question in the context of a restaurant. It’s not necessarily about recreating traditional dishes, but about carrying the influences with you, letting them shape how he cooks and creates new menus with the seasons. In this way, food becomes a subtle but strong link to home, not just rooted in recipes but the expression of taste itself.
