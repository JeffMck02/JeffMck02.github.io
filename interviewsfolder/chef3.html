    Context

    This interview discusses themes of Memory, Multiculturalism, Identity & Understanding why we cook

    Interview Questions/Excerpts

    Can you tell me how you first got started in cooking and what drew you to it in the first place?

    “My parents were nurses, so my house was empty from six in the morning until about nine o’clock at night. All the food had to be prepared by me in regards to myself and my brother, so I had to learn very quickly how not to poison people and how to fully cook food and have good hygiene. I was also a very active kid. I played sports, skateboarded, did lots of outdoor activities, and none of my friends knew how to cook. So friends would come over to my house after school and I would make them food. Whenever I cooked it was never just for myself, it was always for others as well. Eventually I got bored of making the same things and started experimenting.” CONT. “My father was Canadian but his father was a first-generation immigrant from Croatia, so there was a lot of food influence from there as well. When I was with my mother there was Dutch and Indonesian influence in the cooking, and my uncle was Filipino, so going to family gatherings was always very interesting in regards to the types of food I would experience. From an early age I had a very developed palate, and I wasn’t satisfied with very simple things.”

    Are there any dishes or ingredients that are particularly meaningful to you? Why?

    “So it's going to be a funny answer, but soy sauce. I'd say soy sauce is probably the most important ingredient in my cooking career. I think there are very few people on the planet with an understanding of the flavor type called umami. It's the part of your tongue on the left and right side that distinguishes savory flavors from sweet and salty. The general palate of the North American person relies mostly on the front of the tongue sweet and salty flavors. You can see that in fast food habits like McDonald's, Wendy's, Arby's, and things like that. Even Subway bread has a majority sugar content that people would laugh at if they actually saw how much sugar was in it. I find soy sauce is an exceptionally amazing and versatile tool. Its salt and umami profiles, if used conservatively, can add depth and flavor that you wouldn't normally find anywhere else. It brings out spices, sweetness, even color. It provides such a dynamic response in almost any situation that I think it should be a staple in every kitchen. Obviously when mixed with things like plain rice or meats you see improvement, but even in things you wouldn't expect, like a fall soup such as pumpkin or butternut squash adding soy sauce with miso, ginger, and garlic can bring out incredible flavor. Once you start experimenting with it you realize how useful it is. What's interesting is that soy sauce appears in cuisines all around the world. One of the chefs I worked with talked about how immigration from China and Japan into South America after World War II created entire fusion cuisines like chifa and nikkei. In Peru you get dishes like lomo saltado or certain ceviches where soy sauce becomes part of the flavor profile. Once you start thinking about it you realize soy sauce shows up everywhere. For such a simple ingredient it provides complex solutions to so many culinary problems. So when you asked the question, it wasn't something I had really thought about consciously before, but when I reflect on it, soy sauce is probably the most influential ingredient in my cooking.”

    Are there any cooking traditions that you feel committed to maintaining?

    “Family dinner is something that I think a lot of people lose focus on. Growing up I used to have dinner with my parents almost every day. When that stopped and everyone started eating alone it changed the family dynamic. When you’re not sitting down together, you’re not talking to each other or sharing what’s going on in your day. Eventually you start to distance yourself. So if there’s one tradition I would keep alive through cooking, it would be family dinner.”

    Commentary

    Ben’s introduction to cooking didn’t come from formal training, but from necessity and exposure. Growing up in a household where he had to cook for himself and his brother, food quickly became something practical, but also social. At the same time though, his experiences with food were shaped by his exposure to a wide range of cultures and influences, through family connections which gave him a diverse palate from a young age. In their work, Geetha Reddy and Rob van Dam talk about how in multicultural societies, food becomes a key place where different cultural traditions meet and interact, shaping how individuals understand taste and identity. This helps explain Ben’s approach to cooking. Rather than being rooted in a single culture or set of traditions, his culinary identity is a product of the diverse environment he grew up in, through exposure, experimentation and the blending of all of his influences growing up.

    In my interview with Ben, we talked a lot about ingredients and how they can influence a dish. One of the ingredients Ben said was most influential in his cooking was soy sauce and what stood out to me in our conversation was not really the ingredient itself but what he had to say about flavor profiles and the ways it can enhance a dish. I've learned a lot about these kinds of things during my time working with Ben. But my conversation with Ben also tells a larger story about how food moves across cultures. Through migration and global exchange, ingredients travel far beyond their origins, becoming part of entirely new or modified culinary traditions. In the late nineteenth century and early twentieth century, particularly after the Second World War, Japanese and Chinese migrants settled in many parts of South America, in this case, Peru. Over time, these migrant communities helped shape the fusion cuisines Ben talks about, where soy sauce eventually became integrated in local dishes, including some Ceviche recipes, which is the national dish of Peru. Arjun Appadurai’s work exploring global cultural flows helps explain this process, showing how cultural practices don’t just stay fixed within their original national boundaries, but move through vast networks of migration, trade and global exchange. As people move, they bring their food practices with them, which then over time become adapted to their new environments and combine with local traditions, This helps give context to how ingredients like soy sauce become embedded in cuisines far from their origin. In places like Peru in this case, these new ingredients are not imported, but reinterpreted, becoming part of entirely new dishes and culinary identities and traditions.

    For Ben, one of the most important traditions to maintain is simple, and it's one I relate to heavily: family dinner. His reflection highlights how shared meals shape relationships, providing space for conversation and connection. He says that when those routines disappear, something changes, not just in eating habits, but in how we relate to each other, especially in families. Margaret Visser argues that meals play a central role in organizing social life, creating regular moments where we come together and reinforce our relationships. Rather than being incidental, these times where we gather together create structured daily routines, giving people a consistent, intentional space to communicate, share experiences and stay connected with each other. This helps explain Ben’s emphasis on family dinner. When those shared meals disappear, it’s not just our eating habits that change, but the structure of our day to day lives and interaction. Without that regular time together, opportunities for conversation and connection start to fade away.
