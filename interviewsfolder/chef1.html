    Context

    Interview Questions/Excerpts

    Can you tell me how you first got started in cooking and what drew you to it in the first place? Are there any particular people or places that shaped how you understand food?

    “My experience with food is definitely a very profound one… it's shaped a large part of my human experience. I'm from Syria, and food is a very important thing in my culture as an Arab. Food has provided me with a lot of safety, especially growing up… there aren't a lot of good memories as a child, but some of the best ones that I do have are the food my grandma would make me. It was some of the most comforting food I could ever have, and I still continue to make her recipes to this day. There's no better memory than remembering my grandma making me a good meal.”

    Are there any dishes or ingredients that are particularly meaningful to you? Why?

    “The dish that's most meaningful to me would be shishbarak… it’s a food that is very exclusive to the Levant, mostly Syria and Lebanon. It was my favorite food growing up and the food my grandma would make on special occasions. It's a dish I've memorized and that I still continue to make for other people to gift them what my grandma gave to me… When I make it for people it's not just showing them me, but showing them where I come from.”

    Are there any dishes you feel are especially tied to your culinary identity or background?

    “Baba ghanoush… literally translates to ‘spoiled father.’ The story goes that there was a king whose birthday was approaching and his people loved him so much they wanted to make the perfect dish to spoil him… They created this dish from roasted eggplant and garlic and presented it to him. The reason I feel like this dish defines the Syrian identity is because we are very hospitable people. We treat our guests like they are the king… we strive to make it about them at that moment.”

    Are there any cooking traditions that you feel committed to maintaining?

    “When we make food in the Syrian tradition it's genuinely about connecting people… We make food not just for the people in our house but for the people in our streets. When people make food they’re thinking of their community and putting their whole hearts into it… No one ever makes food in my community for themselves. We're always making more than necessary because we're always intending to include someone.

    How does cooking connect you to your community?

    “A big part of what it means to come from my community is sharing food. Whenever you make food you're also adding to that tradition. No one ever makes food in my community for themselves. We're always making more than necessary because even if we don't think about it, we're always intending to include someone, even if it's one person or two people or a whole group. A big part of what it means to make food is contributing to the community in that way.”

    Do you see cooking as a way of preserving something important to you or others?

    “Yeah, definitely. I see food as something that preserves community because, as it stands, it gives us something to continue for. We're always looking forward to our next meal because we need it to survive. And so in that way it allows us to engage with our community, and especially when it becomes more of an artistic endeavor and it becomes more aesthetic, we want to also make it meaningful. We're not just cooking to survive. When I make that dish today it's not just preserving my culture, it's preserving the people who have also told similar stories by making that dish, who have also made people happy by feeding them that dish. It's preserving a piece of human history, a piece of human art, a piece of the human experience. Even when I make it now, the dish has evolved so much. Sometimes I twist the recipe or make it in a way that defines me. But when I pass that dish onto my kids, there will still be traces of the people who made it before me. It was the one thing that my grandma passed on to me that I can keep until I die, and it's the one thing I can pass on to my children and to future generations from me. And that makes me happy as a human being.”

    Commentary

    When Rami talks about his grandmother’s cooking, it isn’t just about food to him, it's about the fleeting moments of comfort he could find in his childhood. The meals themselves are something he associates with safety, care and fond memories in ways that go far beyond the dish itself. Food has a very unique ability to hold onto these sorts of experiences and memories. In his chapter in the annual review of anthropology, Jon Holtzman suggests that taste and smell are deeply connected to memory, often bringing us back to vivid moments in a way that feels immediate and physical, rather than something distant. At the same time, these memories are not something static, but recreated when we cook them repeatedly. This concept is something that Anthropologist David Sutton describes in his book Remembrance and Repasts. He also describes memory as something that is lived through everyday practice and experience rather than reminiscing about these moments. In Rami’s case, continuing to cook his grandmother’s recipes is not just about remembering her, but keeping those memories in the present and close to him.

    When Rami talks about making shish barak for others, it's framed in a way that's more than just sharing food, it becomes a way of showing who he is and where he comes from. To him, it carries a sense of personal and cultural identity. In their research on food and identity, Geetha Reddy and Rob van Dam suggest that cooking is one of the main ways that people express and maintain their sense of self, especially in multicultural contexts and environments where identity becomes even more visible What stood out in Rami’s explanation is that identity isn’t just something we feel internally, but something that is performed and shared, in this case through food and sharing, where sharing the dish becomes part of expressing that identity.

    Rami’s explanation of baba ghanoush focuses on hospitality, treating the guest with such care and attention to the point of making them feel like royalty. The story he tells behind the dish reflects this but so does the way he talks about hosting and serving guests. Food scholars like Richard Wilk note that meals often reflect the values of a culture, shaping how people express care, generosity and their identity. In this case, Rami’s story behind baba ghanoush reflects the generosity and hospitality that Rami says encapsulates and defines Syrian identity. In this context, hospitality is not just a gesture, but built into the origin of the dish itself. This shapes how it is prepared, served and shared.

    One of the strongest ideas in Rami’s interview is that food is never made just for the individual. The expectation is always that someone else will be included, whether planned or not. This reflects a more broad understanding of food being something social by nature. Anthropologists Sidney Mintz and Christine Du Bois argue that eating and cooking are shaped by their communities, shared practices and expectations rather than our individual choices. What Rami describes here takes this idea one step further, food becomes a way of maintaining community as well as creating it, where generosity and inclusion are already built into the act of cooking and eating.

    For Rami, passing down these recipes means much more than just keeping the dishes alive, it is about preserving the people and experiences he associates with them. Each time one of these dishes is made, it carries pieces of the people who made it before Rami. Food Scholar Psyche Williams-Forson argues that food practices are a key way that cultural knowledge is maintained and transmitted, especially through everyday acts like cooking and sharing meals. Rather than being formally recorded, this knowledge is passed down through repetition and participation. This concept helps explain what is happening in Rami’s experience of food and recipes. The recipe is not just being remembered, but continually recreated, allowing for the personal memories to persist but also cultural tradition and meaning. In this way, the recipe is something that far outlasts the individuals that make it, linking the past and the future through an everyday act like cooking.
